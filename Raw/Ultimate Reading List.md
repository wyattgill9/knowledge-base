A unified curriculum combining a chronological Western philosophy track with broader explorations in literature, science, psychology, theology, history, and social theory. Each phase builds on the last. Spend roughly the time indicated, but go at your own pace. The key is reading deeply, not rushing.

---

# PART I: PHILOSOPHY

---

## PHASE 1: FOUNDATIONS (Months 1–6)

Learn *how* philosophers think, get comfortable with the major questions, and build a map of the terrain.

**Orientation texts** (read in this order):

1. **Sophie's World** — Jostein Gaarder. A novel that walks you through the entire history of Western philosophy. The perfect on-ramp.
2. **The Problems of Philosophy** — Bertrand Russell. Short, elegant, and teaches you what philosophy actually *does*.
3. **What Does It All Mean?** — Thomas Nagel. A brief, accessible introduction to the central questions.
4. **Think: A Compelling Introduction to Philosophy** — Simon Blackburn. Covers the core problems (knowledge, mind, free will, ethics, God) clearly and fairly.
5. **The History of Western Philosophy** — Bertrand Russell. Biased but brilliant. Gives you the full narrative arc from the Pre-Socratics to the 20th century. Read it as a story, not as gospel.

**Logic** (start early, continue throughout):

6. **Logic: A Very Short Introduction** — Graham Priest, or **forall x** — P.D. Magnus (free online). Return to Priest's *An Introduction to Non-Classical Logic* in later phases.

---

## PHASE 2: THE ANCIENTS (Months 6–12)

The Greeks set the agenda for everything that follows. This is non-negotiable foundation.

**Pre-Socratics** (read *about* them before reading fragments):

1. **The Pre-Socratic Philosophers** — Jonathan Barnes. The best scholarly introduction.
2. Fragments of **Heraclitus** and **Parmenides** — via Kirk, Raven & Schofield's collection.

**Plato** (read in this order):

3. **Euthyphro** — What is piety? Your first real Socratic dialogue.
4. **Apology** — Socrates' trial. One of the most important texts in all of philosophy.
5. **Meno** — Can virtue be taught? Introduces the theory of recollection.
6. **Phaedo** — The immortality of the soul.
7. **Symposium** — On love. Beautiful and profound.
8. **The Republic** — The masterpiece. Justice, the ideal state, the allegory of the cave, the Forms. Take your time.
9. **Phaedrus** — The soul, rhetoric, and writing.
10. **Theaetetus** — What is knowledge? Foundational for epistemology.
11. **Parmenides**, **Sophist**, **Timaeus** — Advanced Plato. Return to these after Phase 4 if they feel impenetrable now.

**Aristotle** (read in this order):

12. **Nicomachean Ethics** — What is the good life? One of the greatest books ever written on ethics.
13. **Politics** (Books I–III at minimum)
14. **Poetics** — On tragedy, narrative, and art.
15. **Categories** and **De Interpretatione** — Your first taste of logic and metaphysics.
16. **Physics** (Books I–II) and **Metaphysics** (Books I, IV, VII) — Difficult but essential. Use a companion guide.
17. **De Anima** — On the soul. Advanced; revisit later if needed.

**Hellenistic Philosophy:**

18. **Letters and Principal Doctrines** — Epicurus
19. **Discourses and Enchiridion** — Epictetus (Stoicism)
20. **Meditations** — Marcus Aurelius

**Later Antiquity (advanced):**

21. **Plotinus: Enneads** (selections) — The summit of Neoplatonism. Return to this after medieval philosophy if preferred.

---

## PHASE 3: MEDIEVAL & EARLY MODERN (Months 12–18)

### Medieval

1. **Confessions** — Augustine. The birth of introspection in Western thought.
2. **The Consolation of Philosophy** — Boethius
3. **Summa Theologica** (selected questions: on God's existence, on the nature of law) — Thomas Aquinas. Use Peter Kreeft's *Summa of the Summa* as a guided selection.

### The Early Modern Revolution

This is where philosophy as we know it takes shape.

4. **The Prince** — Niccolò Machiavelli. Politics stripped of moralism.
5. **Meditations on First Philosophy** — René Descartes. "I think, therefore I am." One of the most important texts you will ever read.
6. **Three Dialogues Between Hylas and Philonous** — George Berkeley. Idealism at its most vivid.
7. **Ethics** — Baruch Spinoza. Demanding but extraordinary. God, nature, and freedom, laid out in geometric proofs. Use a guide like Nadler's.
8. **An Essay Concerning Human Understanding** (abridged) — John Locke
9. **Second Treatise of Government** — John Locke
10. **Leviathan** — Thomas Hobbes
11. **The Social Contract** — Jean-Jacques Rousseau
12. **A Treatise of Human Nature** (Book I) — David Hume. The most important empiricist text ever written.
13. **An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding** — David Hume. More accessible version of his key ideas.
14. **Monadology** and **Discourse on Metaphysics** — Gottfried Leibniz

---

## PHASE 4: KANT AND THE GERMAN TRADITION (Months 18–24)

This is the mountain. Kant changed everything. You cannot master philosophy without mastering Kant.

1. **Prolegomena to Any Future Metaphysics** — Kant. Read this *before* the Critique. It's Kant explaining his own system in accessible terms.
2. **Critique of Pure Reason** — Kant. Use the Cambridge edition (Guyer & Wood translation). Supplement with Sebastian Gardner's *Kant and the Critique of Pure Reason*.
3. **Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals** — Kant. His moral philosophy. Clear, powerful, and foundational.
4. **Critique of Practical Reason** — Kant
5. **Critique of the Power of Judgment** — Kant. Aesthetics and teleology.

**Post-Kantian Idealism:**

6. **Phenomenology of Spirit** — G.W.F. Hegel. The hardest book on this list. Use Peter Singer's *Hegel: A Very Short Introduction* first, then attempt the Preface and the master-slave dialectic with a commentary (try Stephen Houlgate's guide).
7. **Philosophy of Right** — Hegel. His political philosophy.
8. **The World as Will and Representation** (Vol. 1) — Arthur Schopenhauer. A magnificent writer and Kant's most savage critic.

**Advanced (return later):**

9. **Science of Logic** — Hegel. Only after serious engagement with the *Phenomenology*.

---

## PHASE 5: THE 19TH-CENTURY DISRUPTORS (Months 24–30)

1. **On Liberty** and **Utilitarianism** — John Stuart Mill
2. **Fear and Trembling** — Søren Kierkegaard
3. **The Sickness Unto Death** — Kierkegaard
4. **Either/Or** (selections) — Kierkegaard
5. **Beyond Good and Evil** — Friedrich Nietzsche
6. **On the Genealogy of Morality** — Nietzsche. One of the most electrifying books in philosophy.
7. **The Gay Science** — Nietzsche
8. **Thus Spoke Zarathustra** — Nietzsche (read after the others; it's literary philosophy)
9. **Capital** (Vol. 1, selections: chapters 1, 6, 10, 25–33) — Karl Marx
10. **The German Ideology** (Part 1) — Marx & Engels
11. **Pragmatism** — William James

**Advanced Kierkegaard:**

12. **Concluding Unscientific Postscript** — Kierkegaard

## PHASE 6: 20TH-CENTURY CONTINENTAL (Months 30–36)

**Entry points:**

1. **Existentialism Is a Humanism** — Jean-Paul Sartre. A short, accessible lecture.
2. **The Myth of Sisyphus** — Albert Camus
3. **The Question Concerning Technology** — Martin Heidegger (essay; good entry point before *Being and Time*)
4. **Philosophy of Existence** — Karl Jaspers

**Core texts:**

5. **Being and Time** (Division I) — Heidegger. Use Hubert Dreyfus's commentary *Being-in-the-World* alongside it.
6. **Being and Nothingness** (Introduction and Part I) — Sartre
7. **Phenomenology of Perception** (Introduction and Part I) — Maurice Merleau-Ponty
8. **The Second Sex** — Simone de Beauvoir
9. **The Human Condition** — Hannah Arendt
10. **The Origins of Totalitarianism** — Arendt
11. **Discipline and Punish** — Michel Foucault
12. **The History of Sexuality** (Vol. 1) — Foucault
13. **Of Grammatology** (Part I) — Jacques Derrida
14. **Totality and Infinity** — Emmanuel Levinas

**Advanced:**

15. **Contributions to Philosophy (Of the Event)** — Heidegger. Late Heidegger; only after thorough engagement with *Being and Time*.
16. **Difference and Repetition** — Gilles Deleuze
17. **Cartesian Meditations** and **Ideas I** — Edmund Husserl. The phenomenological foundations behind much of this phase.

---

## PHASE 7: 20TH-CENTURY ANALYTIC (Months 36–42)

1. **Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus** — Ludwig Wittgenstein
2. **Philosophical Investigations** — Wittgenstein. Read this slowly. It will change how you think about language, meaning, and mind.
3. **The Foundations of Arithmetic** — Gottlob Frege
4. **"On Sense and Reference"** and **"The Thought"** — Frege (essays)
5. **"On Denoting"** — Bertrand Russell (essay)
6. **Language, Truth and Logic** — A.J. Ayer
7. **"Two Dogmas of Empiricism"** — W.V.O. Quine (essay)
8. **Word and Object** (selections) — Quine
9. **Naming and Necessity** — Saul Kripke
10. **"What Is It Like to Be a Bat?"** — Thomas Nagel (essay)
11. **Reasons and Persons** — Derek Parfit. A masterpiece on personal identity and ethics.
12. **A Theory of Justice** — John Rawls. The most important work of political philosophy in the 20th century.

---

## PHASE 8: ADVANCED & CONTEMPORARY PHILOSOPHY (Months 42–54+)

By now you'll have strong instincts about what draws you. Here are the major branches:

**Epistemology:** Timothy Williamson, *Knowledge and Its Limits*; Laurence BonJour, *The Structure of Empirical Knowledge*

**Philosophy of Mind:** David Chalmers, *The Conscious Mind*; Daniel Dennett, *Consciousness Explained*; Jerry Fodor, *The Language of Thought*

**Ethics & Metaethics:** Christine Korsgaard, *The Sources of Normativity*; Bernard Williams, *Ethics and the Limits of Philosophy*; G.E.M. Anscombe, "Modern Moral Philosophy"; Alasdair MacIntyre, *After Virtue*

**Political Philosophy:** Robert Nozick, *Anarchy, State, and Utopia*; Charles Mills, *The Racial Contract*; Martha Nussbaum, *Frontiers of Justice*; Rawls, *Political Liberalism*; Habermas, *The Theory of Communicative Action*

**Philosophy of Language:** Donald Davidson, *Inquiries into Truth and Interpretation*; J.L. Austin, *How to Do Things with Words*

**Philosophy of Science:** Thomas Kuhn, *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*; Karl Popper, *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*; Bas van Fraassen, *The Scientific Image*; Imre Lakatos, *Proofs and Refutations*; Paul Feyerabend, *Against Method*

**Philosophy of Mathematics:** Euclid, *Elements* (Books I–IV); Russell & Whitehead, *Principia Mathematica* (introduction + selections); Gödel's incompleteness papers (with commentary)

**Process Philosophy:** Alfred North Whitehead, *Process and Reality*

# A SUGGESTED YEAR-BY-YEAR PATH

**Year 1:** Foundations & orientation → Dostoevsky → The Greeks (Plato's *Republic*, Aristotle's *Ethics*) → Stoics & Epicureans → Begin science reading

**Year 2:** Medieval & Early Modern (Descartes through Hume) → Nietzsche → Camus & Sartre → Begin literature track (Kafka, Borges, Camus fiction)

**Year 3:** Kant (all three Critiques) → Schopenhauer → Kierkegaard → Mill & political philosophy → Psychology (Freud, Jung)

**Year 4:** Hegel → Marx → Heidegger (*Being and Time*) → Wittgenstein → Theology track

**Year 5:** Deep literature (Proust, Mann, Joyce) → 20th-century analytic philosophy → Philosophy of science → History & grand theory

**Ongoing:** Economics, social theory, science, and mathematics woven throughout. Return to the hardest texts for rereading.

# HOW TO ACTUALLY DO THIS

**Read with a pen.** Annotate everything. Write summaries of each chapter in your own words. If you can't explain what an argument says, you don't understand it yet.

**Read secondary literature.** For every major philosopher (Plato, Aristotle, Descartes, Kant, Hegel, Nietzsche, Wittgenstein, Heidegger), read at least one good companion or commentary alongside the primary text. The *Cambridge Companion to X* and *Very Short Introduction* series are consistently excellent.

**Write.** After each phase, try writing a 2–3 page essay on a question that interests you. This forces clarity.

**Re-read.** The *Republic*, Descartes' *Meditations*, the *Critique of Pure Reason*, *Philosophical Investigations*, and *Being and Time* will all reward three or four readings across your life.

**Don't skip the hard ones.** Kant, Hegel, Heidegger, and Wittgenstein will feel impossible at first. That's normal. Push through with commentaries. The difficulty is where the growth happens.

**The point isn't to consume but to metabolize.** The difficulty isn't an obstacle — it's the point. What resists you transforms you.
